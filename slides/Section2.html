  • The fundamental building block of hierarchical models is conditional probability, and appropriate probability distributions.

Probability Distributions

  • Probability distributions can have different interpretations, here we prefer to consider them a way of quantifying uncertainty about unknown quantities.
  • Consider that probability distributions depend on parameters. We can think of such a distribution as being "conditional" on some fixed value of those parameters.

Modeling, Hierarchical Models, and Bayesian Inference

Probability Distributions

  • A single level model relates a "random" outcome to explanatory information.
  • Examples: linear and logistic regression.
  • Hierarchical models address additional levels of uncertainty, or randomness.
  • Different terminology is often used depending on the problem context.
  • The underlying concept is the same: we need models with additional flexibility in order to better match the structure of the data.

Modeling, Hierarchical Models, and Bayesian Inference

Building Hierarchical Models - General Process

  1. Consider the process of interest and design a reasonable model under the assumption that important quantities are known.
  2. Identify the available data, examine how it relates to this process, and confirm that it is sufficient for modeling (sometimes via trial and error).
  3. Quantify the uncertainty about all remaining model parameters.
  4. Fit the model using appropriate software.
  5. Sanity check the results, and apply them to the desired outcome.

Hierarchical Models - Potential Drawbacks

  1. Computational complexity
  2. Implicit assumptions
  3. Identifiability concerns
  4. Need for careful interpretation, and the potential for misapplication by businesses and policymakers

SEIR Models

General Structure

  1. Susceptible: capable of contracting an infection
  2. Exposed: infected, but not yet capable of spreading a disease
  3. Infectious: able to pass a disease on to others
  4. Removed: no longer infectious, either by recovery and subsequent immunity, or death

SEIR Models

General Structure

  • We're particularly interested in stochastic SEIR models, as opposed to versions build on ordinary and partial differential equations.
  • These models are defined for discrete spatial units and time points.
  • Additional assumptions induced by the structure of the model include exponentially distributed compartment membership times.
